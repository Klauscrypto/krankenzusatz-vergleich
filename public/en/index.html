GKV vs. Reality

What your German public health insurance actually covers

Germany's public health insurance (GKV – gesetzliche Krankenversicherung) is among the best in the world for basic care. But "basic" is the key word. As an expat you'll quickly discover the gaps when you visit a dentist, need a specialist fast, or require treatment abroad.

A supplemental health insurance plan (Zusatzversicherung) sits on top of your GKV and covers exactly what the state doesn't. You keep your GKV — you just add targeted protection where it matters.

Good news: you don't need to speak fluent German to apply. Most top insurers accept English applications, and our comparison is fully in English with personal English-speaking advisors.

Expat FAQ

Everything expats ask about German health insurance

GKV (gesetzliche Krankenversicherung) is Germany's statutory public health insurance. If you are employed in Germany earning under approximately €73,800/year (2026 threshold), you are required by law to be insured through GKV. Your employer pays roughly half the monthly premium. GKV covers doctor visits, hospital stays and some medications — but there are significant gaps, especially for dental and private specialist care.
Yes, absolutely. Any legal resident of Germany can purchase supplemental health insurance regardless of nationality. You typically need: a German address, a German IBAN for premium payment, and to already be insured under GKV (or PKV). Nationality is not a barrier. Most top insurers accept English-language applications online.
GKV = Public health insurance. Mandatory for most employed people earning under the income threshold. Covers basic care.

PKV = Full private insurance. Available to high earners and the self-employed. Replaces GKV entirely — more expensive, more comprehensive.

Zusatzversicherung = Supplemental insurance. You keep your GKV but add specific top-up coverage (dental, hospital, etc.). This is what most expats on GKV use to fill the gaps without switching to expensive PKV.
It depends on the plan. Many providers offer plans with no waiting period at all — especially if you apply before any treatment is underway. Some plans have a 3-month general waiting period and up to 8 months for dental. Tip: apply as soon as you arrive in Germany, before you need treatment. Our comparison clearly shows which plans offer immediate coverage.
Standard supplemental plans cover you in Germany only. For international coverage you need a separate international travel health insurance (Auslandskrankenversicherung). The good news: these are incredibly affordable — annual worldwide plans for a single person start from around €8–30/year. Many expats combine a dental top-up for Germany with a travel plan for trips abroad.
